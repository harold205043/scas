Line Work

Tattoo line work is a foundational skill that every tattoo artist should master. It forms the outline of a design and sets the stage for the shading and coloring stages. Perfecting your line work is crucial for creating clean, precise, and long-lasting tattoos.

Learn More

Shading

Tattoo shading is an essential technique that adds depth, dimension, and texture to a design. Whether it's creating smooth gradients or bold contrasts, mastering shading techniques is vital for bringing tattoos to life. Executing flawless shading requires skill, practice, and an understanding of light and shadow.

Learn More

Color Packing

Color packing is a fundamental skill in tattooing that involves efficiently and consistently filling in outlined areas with solid, vibrant colors. Mastering color packing techniques is crucial for achieving even, rich color saturation in tattoos. This skill requires precision, an understanding of color theory, and the ability to manipulate tattoo machines effectively.

About

Applying a tattoo stencil is a crucial first step in creating a precise and visually appealing tattoo. The stencil acts as a guide for the tattoo artist, outlining the design on the client's skin before the actual tattooing begins. Properly transferring the stencil ensures that the tattoo design is positioned accurately, helping the artist maintain the desired proportions and symmetry.

"COLOR Packing" 

          
           Before starting the color packing process for a tattoo, it is important to 
            be familiar with fundamental techniques and guidelines:
            



            1.Small Concentric Circles Technique:

            Begin the process with small concentric circles, avoiding putting too much color, as commonly done on
            drawing paper. The main goal of color packing in this technique is to cover rounds and small groupings
            of mags. 



            2.The Push Technique: 

            This method involves moving the tattoo machine back and forth across the skin surface, similar to a
            pendulum, to help the deep penetration of pigment into the skin in one fluid motion. 


             3.The Pull Technique: 

            When enhanced shading is required, the Pull Technique comes into play. It involves pulling the tattoo
            machine away from the tube tip, preventing an excessive flow of tattoo ink from penetrating deeply into
            the skin. 


            Key Guidelines for Color Packing Tattoos. 


             1.Machine: 

            Color packing relies on the tattoo machine, with similar machines being applicable for shading purposes.
            It is essential to adjust the shader and voltage based on the skin type and tone. For example, a direct
            hit is ideal for packing solid colors, while a shallower stroke is used for layering black and grays.

            Recommended Machine for Color Packing Tattoo: Dragonhawk Rotary Tattoo Pen Machine Kit. 


             2.Speed:

            Adjusting the speed of the tattoo machine depends on factors such as the body part, tattoo area, and
            design type. Liners operate at a consistent speed, while the machine's speed must be reduced for
            layering colors or gray. 


             3.Tattoo Area:

            Shading begins after the completion of the design outline. Thoroughly cleaning the entire outlined area
            is strongly recommended before starting the shading process, to eliminate any remaining unwanted ink
            from the outlining phase. This cleaning also helps to remove stencil marks and other remaining sticky
            ink adhered to the skin. 


             4.Handling Inks:

            Packing black tattoo ink is achieved through consistent hand movements. The process remains consistent
            for solid colors. Achieving seamless color blends requires a reduction in machine speed and relaxation
            of the stretched area to yield clean edges, paving the way for the subsequent phase of color.

"Items Needed for Setting Up a Tattoo Station"


  1. Gloves
  2. Bleach
  3. Mop
  4. Madacide (in a spray bottle)
  5. Paper towels
  6. Massage table
  7. Rolling stool or chair
  8. Tattoo tray
  9. Lamp
  10. Power supply
  11. Foot pedal (if using coil machines)

  1. After washing your hands with antibacterial soap, put on a fresh pair of gloves and spray down all furniture and your tray with Madacide. This solution will kill germs and viruses and needs to be left for 3 minutes before wiping it away with clean paper towels. In the meantime, mop the floor of your workstation with a bleach-and-water solution.

    2. Note: A personal studio should only have non-porous furniture. Use metal folding chairs or a massage table instead of a wooden or cushioned chair. Use a metal table and tray instead of a wood or plastic workspace. Never use carpet in a private studio. Make sure your floors are made of a non-porous material and are easy to clean (like tile).
  2. Step 2: Set Up Your Barriers

    3. You will need gloves, plastic barriers (plastic wrap), tape, barrier film sheets, and dental bib.
    When setting up your barriers, you need to consider where you’ll be placing your materials and what you’ll be touching during the tattoo process. Make sure to wrap the entire massage table with plastic wrap, even if you’re only going to be working on a small area. Make sure you put on a fresh pair of gloves before setting up your barriers.
    First, you need to cover your entire tray (whether you’re using the top of a non-porous tool box or a stainless steel dental tray) with plastic wrap. Make sure the plastic wrap reaches down and around all the edges of the tray and that any separate pieces overlap one another. You can use tape to secure the plastic wrap. Next, place a dental bib on top of the wrapped area and secure with tape. The dental bib should have one waxy side and one softer side. The waxy side faces down.
  3. Step 3: Set Up Your Tray

    4. You will need tincture of Green Soap (1/10 solution), distilled water, 70% Isopropyl Rubbing Alcohol, squeeze bottles, plastic rinse cup, ink caps, paper towels, razor, popsicle stick, Vaseline, extra rubber bands (if using a coil tattoo machine), tattoo needles, and extra Nitrile Gloves.
    On your station, set up two squeeze bottles. The first will contain a 1/10 solution of tincture of green soap and distilled water. The green soap bottle will be used to clean the skin before shaving. The Isopropyl rubbing alcohol will be used to strip the skin of oils after shaving. Place an unused disposable razor on your dental bib. Make sure the guard stays on until you use it and place the guard back on after. Fill a plastic rinse cup about 3/4 full with distilled water. You can dip the tip of your needle in the water while tattooing to clean it out before using another color. Using a popsicle stick (to avoid cross-contamination), scoop a generous amount of Vaseline and place it on the dental bib. Tear as many paper towels as you think you’ll need and set them on your tray. Place an ink cap on your tray for each color and grey wash you’ll be using. Fill each ink cap. You will also need to know how many needles your tattoo design requires before tattooing. Plan out what you’ll need in advance for both your line work and shading, and place those needles on your tray.
  4. Step 4: Set up Your Tattoo Machine
    5. You will need tattoo machine(s), disposable grip, traditional needle bar or needle cartridges, clip cord (coil machines) or RCA cord (rotary machines), machine bags, clip cord cover, and medical tape.
    How you set up your tattoo machines will depend on whether you are using a coil, rotary, or pen machine. However, no matter what machine you’re using, you need to make sure to cover your machine with a machine bag. You can then secure the bag using medical tape or grip tape. You must use disposable grips if you do not have an autoclave. Next, slide your RCA cord or clip cord through a clip cord cover and connect it to your machine and power supply. Place your machine on the tray with the needle facing away from you until you’re ready to use it. Never leave a tattoo machine on the tray with the needle facing you or hanging off the side of your tray; doing so increases the risk of a needle stick injury. 


          
 Shading

          
 
            When shading a tattoo, there are a few techniques you can use to achieve different effects:

          1. Tattoo Shading:
               - To create solid fills in areas with darker shading, move the needle in tight oval motions over the skin to cover more surface 
               area and prevent gaps from appearing in your shading.
               - Use larger mags to cover a big space and slow down your work and tattoo machine speed to pack the black pigment into the skin.
               - While shading, flow with the piece and continually expand the shading of the area you're working on until the piece is filled.
            
            2. Whip Shading:
               - This technique leaves a dark mark on the skin trailed by a lighter gradient.
               - To do it, touch the needle into the skin, then drag it across, and gently flick the machine away from the skin.
               - With whip shading, move from your solid line to light, peppery dots, and do not press too hard into the skin.
               - After whipping shading in one direction, turn the mag to go another way to make the shading effect even in all 
               directions as you blend out to skin tone.
            
            3. Pendulum Shading:
               - This technique gives you a dark mark on the skin with lighter gradients on either side by moving the needle back and 
               forth gradually in and out of the skin to distribute less tattoo ink, then more ink, and then less ink again, leaving a 
               smooth gradient in the skin with a “feathered edge.”
               - When feathering out the shading, use the same motion with a lighter gray wash to build off the darker shading.
            
            There are also advanced techniques like Stipple Shading, Brush Shading, and techniques to fix common tattoo shading mistakes. 
            Remember that using these techniques, along with paying attention to common mistakes and their fixes, can help you create smooth
